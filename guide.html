The system at a glance

CODE — my four-step loop

Everything I read, hear, or study passes through this loop. The goal is not to store more — it's to make knowledge usable for my art, my work, and my thinking.

C
Capture
Save what resonates. One tool per format.
O
Organize
Route by actionability, not subject.
D
Distill
Compress in layers. Add my own voice.
E
Express
Turn notes into art, writing, work.
The one rule I return to: Capture what resonates, not everything. Resonance is the filter. If I'm not sure, I don't save it.

Step one

Capture

My only job here is to notice what resonates and get it out of my head before I lose it. I don't evaluate or organise at capture time — that comes later.

The resonance test: Would I want to remember this in five years? Would it change how I think, make, or work? If yes — capture it. If not — let it go.
📖Physical books

I don't annotate while reading — it breaks flow. Instead: sticky flags for pages to return to, then a 3-minute "book download" at the end of each session.

Book download = voice memo or typed note: what landed, what surprised me, what it connects to. Done.

Voice Memos → Obsidian inbox
📱Kindle & Kobo

Highlight freely. Add a note to any highlight I want to develop — even just a question mark or "!!" to mark it as important.

Readwise syncs everything automatically. I don't touch the highlights until Distill time.

Readwise → Obsidian (auto)
🎧Audiobooks

Timestamp rule: when something strikes me, I pause and record: "[timestamp] — [idea] — because [why it matters to me]."

The "because" is non-negotiable. It forces me to make the idea mine in the moment.

Voice memo → manual Obsidian note
🎙Podcasts

Snipd: press the clip button at any moment worth keeping. It transcribes automatically. I add a 1-line reaction before moving on.

Syncs to Readwise → Obsidian without any extra work.

Snipd → Readwise → Obsidian (auto)
🌐Articles & web

I don't read articles as they appear in my feeds. I save them to Readwise Reader first, then read in a dedicated batch. Highlights sync automatically.

For full pages: Obsidian web clipper.

Readwise Reader → Obsidian (auto)
📚Study materials

Feynman capture: I write what I just learned in my own words immediately, as if explaining it to someone unfamiliar with the subject.

Tagged #study and filed under the active project or Area in Obsidian.

Manual note → tagged #study

What I don't do

I don't capture everything. I don't highlight whole paragraphs "just in case." I don't save articles I haven't decided to actually read. The inbox fills up fast enough as it is.

Step two

Organize

I organize by actionability, not subject. The question I ask every note is: what is this for right now? — not what is this about?

P

Projects

Active work with a defined endpoint. A note goes here if it directly serves something I'm doing right now.

  • An essay I'm writing
  • A current art series
  • An active study course
  • A work deliverable
A

Areas

Long-term responsibilities and practices with no end date. I maintain these, I don't complete them.

  • My art practice
  • My professional domain
  • Health, relationships
  • Running reading list
R

Resources

My personal library. Topics I'm curious about, organised by theme. Most reading notes land here.

  • Philosophy / ethics
  • Colour & perception
  • Narrative structure
  • Psychology / cognition
A

Archive

Inactive items from the other three. Not deleted — parked. I can always search back into it.

  • Finished book notes
  • Completed study units
  • Past projects
  • Old reading lists
My filing question: Does this serve an active Project? → Projects. An ongoing Area? → Areas. No active use, but genuinely interesting? → Resources. Everything else goes to Archive or I don't save it.

How I process my inbox (once a week, ~15 min)

Everything captured during the week accumulates in an Inbox folder inside Obsidian. Once a week I process 10–20 items: read, decide, file. I don't try to keep inbox at zero daily — that's too much friction.

My reading list as a Resource

My yearly reading list lives in Resources/Reading as a single note, with books organised by theme, not status. When I start a book, I create a book note linked from this list. When finished, the book note moves to Archive; the reading list stays current.

Step three

Distill

Raw captures are ingredients, not knowledge. Distilling is where I turn a pile of highlights into something I can actually use — and where I add my own voice to what I've read.

1

Raw capture

The highlight, clip, voice note, or photo as captured. I don't edit at this stage. This layer exists automatically — it's everything in my inbox after a reading session.

2

Bold + gloss

When I revisit a note, I bold the single most important idea per passage. Then I add one line in my own words below each bolded section — paraphrase, question, or reaction. This is the most important layer.

3

Summary + my insight

When a note becomes useful for something I'm making, I write a 2–3 sentence summary at the top of the note. Then I add a ✦ Creative connection field: where does this appear in my art or work? What formal, visual, or emotional idea does it touch?

4

Linked to other notes

The best notes start linking outward — [[related concept]], [[book title]], [[project name]]. This is where the network of ideas starts to form and surprising connections emerge.

The distill rule I keep: Add value to a note every time I touch it. Even one line. Even just adding a link. I never distill everything at once — only what I actually need.

My book note template

Every book gets one note. I fill it in progressively — not all at the end of the book.

# Book title — Author Status: Reading / Finished / Archived Finished: YYYY-MM-DD Format: Physical / Kindle / Kobo / Audio Reading list: [[Year Reading List]] Tags: #resource/[theme] ## My 2–3 sentence summary (Written in my own words after finishing) ## ✦ Creative connection Where does this appear in my art or work? What formal / visual / emotional idea does it touch? ## Key ideas (Distilled highlights — bolded core idea, my gloss below each) ## Raw highlights (Synced from Readwise — untouched) ## Questions this book opened (What I now want to investigate further)

Study notes follow a slightly different format — output-focused, since I need to be able to retrieve and apply specific knowledge.

Step four

Express

This is the point. All the capturing, organising, and distilling exists so that when I sit down to make something — a piece of art, an essay, a proposal, a conversation — I'm not starting from nothing.

The shift I make: My Second Brain is not a warehouse. It's a factory. Every note is raw material. I'm always asking: what can I make from what I know?

Intermediate Packets — how I work in small units

Instead of trying to produce a finished work in one sitting, I work in Intermediate Packets — small, completed units that accumulate into larger things.

Packet typeWhat it isExample
Concept noteOne idea, distilled and in my own wordsWhat I understand about negative capability
Connection noteTwo ideas from different sources, linkedHow this essay on light connects to that novel
Creative seedA phrase, image, or question that could become a workA line from a poem that holds an entire colour for me
Argument draftA partial argument or position, not finishedThree paragraphs on why I make the work I make
Reference setA cluster of notes relevant to one projectFive notes on opacity in painting, pulled together

How I use my notes when making

🎨For art

Search my Resources for the theme I'm working with. Look at the ✦ Creative connection fields. Pull any notes where I've already made the leap from idea to image.

My book notes often contain the best unexpected sources — novels, philosophy, science that I read for curiosity, not for art.

✍️For writing

Search for the topic. Assemble relevant concept notes and connection notes into a scratch document. Write from there — not from a blank page.

The goal is to already have something on the page before I "start writing."

💼For work

Pull the relevant Project and Area notes. Surface any resource notes tagged with the relevant theme. Look for insights from reading that reframe the work problem.

Unexpected cross-domain ideas are the most valuable — something from a novel that clarifies a strategy.

🧠For thinking

Periodic "connection sessions": I open my graph view and look for clusters. What themes keep emerging? What books connect in unexpected ways?

Some of my best insights come not from adding new notes but from looking at what I already have.

The express principle I return to

Information only becomes knowledge when I put it to use. Saving notes is not learning. Making something with them is. I try to produce one Intermediate Packet per reading session — even if it's just a three-sentence connection note.

My weekly review (15 minutes)

Once a week — same time, same place. This is what keeps the system alive.

  1. Open Inbox in Obsidian. Process 10–20 items: read, decide, file into PARA.
  2. Check active Projects: is there anything I read this week that serves a current project? Link it.
  3. Add one layer of distillation to 3–5 notes I've been sitting on.
  4. Look for one connection between two notes from different books or sources. Write a brief connection note if I find one.
  5. Ask: what am I making right now? Are my notes serving it? If not — what do I need?
The review I don't skip: Even if I only process 5 notes and find one connection, the review is worth doing. The system only compounds if I keep the habit.

Tool notes

Obsidian — how I use it

Obsidian stores all notes as plain Markdown files I own. Nothing is locked in a proprietary format. Here are the specific features and plugins I actually use — not a comprehensive list, just the ones that serve this system.

Inside my vault:

Vault/ ├── Inbox/ ← all new captures land here ├── Projects/ ← one folder per active project │ └── Essay on colour/ ├── Areas/ │ ├── Art practice/ │ └── Work/ ├── Resources/ ← my personal library │ ├── Reading/ ← book notes + reading list │ ├── Philosophy/ │ ├── Science/ │ └── Craft/ └── Archive/ ← finished / inactive

Readwise Official — syncs all my Kindle, Kobo, podcast, and article highlights into my Inbox automatically. This is the most important plugin in the system.

Dataview — lets me query my notes like a database. I use it to see all books with status "Reading", or list all notes tagged #study created this month.

Canvas — built into Obsidian. I use this for creative sessions: pull related notes onto a canvas and look for patterns. Useful for art projects and writing planning.

Templates — built in. Stores my book note template and study template so I can create a new note from the template with one command.

Graph View — built in. I open this occasionally (not obsessively) to see which notes are densely linked. The dense nodes are usually my most important ideas.

I use [[double brackets]] to link notes. A few habits:

Every book note links to its author, its themes, and any active project it serves. Every concept note links to the book it came from. When two ideas from different sources connect, I create a small connection note that links both — this is often where the most interesting thinking happens.

I don't link obsessively. I link when a connection is genuinely useful, not to make my graph look impressive.

I keep tags minimal. My main tag system:

#study note from study materials, needs review
#seed a potential creative idea, not yet developed
#question something I want to investigate further
#resource/theme e.g. #resource/philosophy
#review needs another pass of distillation

I don't use tags to replace folders. Folders handle location; tags handle type and status.

Obsidian has a mobile app. I use it for two things only: quick captures to Inbox while reading on my phone, and checking a note I need while away from my desk.

I sync my vault using iCloud (built in) or Obsidian Sync. I don't process or organise notes on mobile — that's a desktop task during my weekly review.

Kobo highlights sync to Readwise via the Readwise browser extension or the Kobo ↔ Readwise direct integration (available in Readwise settings). Once connected, highlights sync automatically whenever my Kobo syncs.

I add a short note to important Kobo highlights before moving on — even just a single word. This makes them much more useful later when they arrive in my Obsidian inbox without any context.

The Obsidian principle I come back to

My notes are plain text files. They'll be readable in 30 years with any text editor. That matters to me. The system is mine — not locked in anyone's platform.
